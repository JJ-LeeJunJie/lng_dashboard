LNG supply failover logic
If spot LNG >$14/MMBtu

Fertiliser plants revert to government APM gas (heavily rationed); power sector switches to coal; industrial users curtail. India's elasticity is the highest of any Northeast Asian buyer.

Domestic production decline

KG-D6 deepwater (ONGC) and Mumbai High mature fields declining ~4–6%/yr post-2025. No near-term replacement. This is the structural LNG demand driver through 2030.

Pipeline connectivity expansion

10,800 km of new pipeline authorised — will unlock CGD demand in East/South India (currently un-served). 2025–27 commissioning of Urja Ganga and Indradhanush grids.

India's gas demand grew at 11.5% CAGR (2022–24) driven by fertiliser subsidies and industrial expansion. Growth will moderate to ~6–8%/yr through 2027 as subsidy policy normalises. The CGD sector (city gas) is the most structural long-run driver — growing from 25% to 32% of gas mix by 2030 as pipeline network expands into un-served cities.

Sector narratives
Fertiliser — 38% of demand

Largest single sector. Government subsidises farmers, bearing ~$30Bn/yr in fertiliser subsidies (FY2023). This makes fertiliser plants near price-insensitive to LNG spot prices. India's entire LNG demand growth since FY2016 is attributable almost entirely to this sector. RLNG for urea plants is the key demand lever.

Power — 17% of demand (declining)

8 GW of gas-fired capacity retired in April 2025 after generating zero electricity for years — a historic stranded asset event. Gas-fired power cannot compete with coal (~$6/MMBtu) or renewables. 31 plants across 8GW operated at below 10% load factor Nov 2024–Mar 2025. Power sector LNG is a story of decline.

CGD / city gas — 20% & growing

CNG vehicles (transport) + residential / commercial gas connections. Growing as new pipeline coverage unlocks cities. CGD sector consumes ~40 MMscmd/day. EV penetration is beginning to challenge CNG — EV sales grew 123% faster than CNG vehicles over the past 8 years. CGD remains growth story to 2030.

India's LNG demand has four key monthly trigger windows. The JKM price level is the overarching signal — it determines whether India buys spot or diverts demand to coal and APM gas.
Apr–Jun
Pre-monsoon power demand + summer industrial ramp

Watch: JKM spot price; CEA power shortage data; RLNG uptake at gas plants.
Bull: JKM <$11 → RLNG power plants operating above 20% load factor; industrial gas demand picking up.
Bear: JKM >$14 → gas plants idle; coal restores full dispatch; spot cargo purchases cancelled.

Jul–Sep
Kharif fertiliser season + monsoon industrial slowdown

Watch: Govt fertiliser allocation announcements; urea import tender volumes (inverse indicator for RLNG).
Bull: Strong kharif season → fertiliser plants at max capacity; RLNG demand elevated despite monsoon.
Bear: Weak monsoon + low farmer income → govt reduces urea subsidy allocation → fertiliser LNG cut.

Oct–Nov
Rabi season + winter pre-positioning

Watch: Petronet Dahej booking rate; spot cargo tenders by GAIL/IOC; CGD offtake in northern cities.
Bull: Cold north Indian winter arriving early → residential CGD + industrial heat demand spikes; buyers pre-position.
Bear: Global LNG market tightens (Europe winter demand) → JKM spikes; India pulls back from spot market.

Dec–Feb
Winter peak + Dahej utilisation signal

Watch: Dahej regasification utilisation (real-time capacity signal); LNG cargo spot price India landed cost.
Bull: Dahej at >90% → market tight; other terminals absorbing incremental cargoes; new pipeline commissioning unlocking eastern demand.
Bear: JKM >$16 → India cancels spot tenders, diverts contracted cargoes to Europe for premium; domestic shortfall managed by curtailment.

Nuclear restart → LNG displacement
Kashiwazaki-Kariwa Unit 6 restart (Feb 2026)

1,356 MW displaces ~1.3 mmt/yr of LNG (62 Bcf/yr). Unit 7 (1,356 MW) delayed to 2029–30. Each 1 GW of nuclear restart at 80% capacity factor ≈ ~0.9 mmt/yr of LNG demand displaced.

Structural over-contracting risk

IEEFA estimates Japanese utilities may be over-contracted by up to 12 mmt/yr as domestic demand falls. Companies are resorting to overseas resales (now 40% of handled volumes) to manage the imbalance.

Seasonal swing buffer

Japan's LNG storage (~3.5 Mt) can cover ~3 weeks of peak winter demand. One very cold day in Tokyo consumes ~65,000 tonnes — a full cargo. Winter supply disruptions move JKM within days.

Japan's LNG imports declined for the 9th consecutive year in FY2025, reaching ~62 mmt vs 75 mmt in FY2019. Gas-fired power (33% of electricity in 2024) is the sole swing sector — power demand is structurally displaced by nuclear and renewables. City gas and industrial demand are relatively stable. Japan's gas demand will not recover; the debate is about the rate of decline.

Demand by sector — sub-components
Power — 57% of gas demand

Gas accounted for 33% of Japan's total electricity in 2024, down from 42% peak in 2014. Nuclear restarts structurally displace gas — each reactor adds ~0.9 mmt/yr of LNG displacement. 15 reactors online (33 GW) as of Feb 2026. Target: 20% nuclear by 2040, requiring ~30 reactors. Base case: 20–22 reactors by 2030.

City gas — 21% (stable)

Residential heating, cooking, commercial. Relatively price-inelastic. Mildly declining as efficient appliances and electrification replace gas in households. Tokyo Gas and Osaka Gas are dominant distributors. Market liberalisation (2017 Gas Business Act) introduced competition but volumes remain stable.

Industrial — 16% (stable/slight decline)

Chemicals, steel, glass, food. Relatively stable demand base. Some industrial coal-to-gas switching has occurred but is limited by LNG import prices. Japan's industrial sector is mature; there is no significant new capacity coming online that would boost gas demand.

Japan as LNG trader — the re-sell story
Key infrastructure

Japan operates over 30 LNG import terminals with ~250 MMtpa total nameplate capacity — far exceeding actual imports of ~62 mmt. Major hubs: Futtsu (JERA), Sodegaura (Tokyo Gas), Himeji (Kansai Electric), Senboku (Osaka Gas). Liquefaction capacity for domestic storage re-injection exceeds 80 Mt. Japan's storage (~3.5 Mt) is LNG in regasification state — cryogenic tanks at coastal terminals, not underground caverns. The world's most sophisticated and redundant LNG infrastructure network.

Japan's LNG demand is driven by three swing variables: winter temperatures, nuclear availability, and summer cooling demand. Unlike India, Japan is price-inelastic for term supply — price matters mainly for resale decisions, not domestic consumption levels.
Dec–Feb
Winter peak — the make-or-break period

Watch: Tokyo temperature anomaly vs 30-yr average; JERA/Tokyo Gas inventory levels; nuclear outage announcements.
Bull: Cold snap + nuclear outage → emergency cargo purchases; JKM spot spike >$15; Japan becomes net buyer rather than re-seller. Historically: 1°C colder than average = +65,000 t LNG demand in Tokyo region alone.
Bear: Warm winter + all planned reactors running → re-sales surge; re-sale volumes push Asia spot prices down.

Jul–Sep
Summer cooling + nuclear maintenance season

Watch: Electricity generation data; nuclear capacity factor; solar output (Japan's solar ~90GW; high generation in clear summers depresses gas).
Bull: Hot summer + planned nuclear maintenance → gas-fired dispatch rises; 2025 pattern (summer surpassed winter gas demand) could repeat.
Bear: Mild summer + full nuclear + strong solar → gas power dispatch near historic lows; re-sales maximised.

Why Korea is bullish (unlike Japan)
Nuclear retirements outpacing restarts (2025–2029)

Hanbit No.1 went offline Dec 2025. Multiple aging units scheduled for decommissioning. New builds (Saeul 3&4) delayed. Net nuclear capacity is DECLINING in the near term, creating a gas power gap.

East coast grid congestion restricting coal

East coast transmission line to metropolitan area delayed beyond original 2027 to now expected 2029. Coal plants restricted year-round. This is a persistent bullish driver for gas through 2029.

Fine dust management — seasonal coal shutdowns

Government 7th Seasonal Fine Dust Plan shuts 17 coal plants Dec–Mar (up from 15 in prior season). This creates a structural winter gas demand uplift every year until coal retirement is complete.

Korean gas demand seasonality is shifting — from winter-dominant residential heating (legacy) toward a bi-seasonal pattern (winter heating + summer cooling). Gas's share of power generation is the key variable: every nuclear outage, every coal restriction, every hot summer day adds LNG demand. S&P Global forecasts LNG imports rising to 55 mmt by 2029 (from 47 mmt in 2025).

Sector narratives
Power — 51% of gas demand

Gas supplied 29% of Korea's electricity in 2024, rising to ~33% by 2029. Key drivers: coal plants shutting (fine dust policy), nuclear delays and retirements, grid congestion on east coast. In Aug 2024, gas-fired power surpassed winter levels for the first time — extreme summer cooling + restricted nuclear. This pattern will repeat.

Industrial — 18% (growing)

Gas demand in the industrial sector grew 4% YOY in 9M 2025 despite slower overall industrial production. Private LNG importers (POSCO, SK Group, GS Group) are increasingly buying direct for their own industrial gas needs. Market liberalisation is accelerating industrial LNG adoption — 25 entities now registered as direct importers.

Residential — 18% (declining)

Historically the dominant sector (45% in early 2000s, now 18%). The shift to electric heating is structural and ongoing. Warmer winters accelerate this trend. Residential sector now a drag on overall LNG growth — any cold winter provides a temporary uplift, but the structural direction is downward. KOGAS bears the brunt as the sole residential supplier.
