China's gas system is structured around three supply tiers: domestic production from western basins (cheapest, policy priority), pipeline imports from Russia and Central Asia (semi-rigid, contract-bound), and LNG imports via coastal terminals (most flexible, most expensive — the swing variable). LNG is priced last, cut first when demand weakens, and purchased aggressively when markets tighten.

China's gas demand maps onto four macro-regions totalling 430 Bcm. The coastal East + South belt (59%) is served primarily by LNG imports. North China is the most seasonal market (3–4x winter/summer swing), increasingly supplied by Russian POS-1. The Southwest is unique — Sichuan is both China's top shale gas producer and a major power-sector consumer.

Supply failover logic
What happens when each source fails
LNG disruption (Hormuz closure / terminal outage)

Activate underground gas storage (UGS) withdrawal; boost domestic production; coal substitution in power dispatch; delay storage injections; redirect POS-1 south; throttle re-exports.

Central Asia pipeline disruption (CAGP cut)

Increase LNG spot purchases via coastal terminals; draw on Xinjiang domestic fields; reroute West-East Pipeline flow. Kazakhstan cut all exports in March 2025 — LNG filled within days.

Hydro shortfall (summer drought — Sichuan/Yunnan)

Dispatch gas-fired peakers (Guangdong, Jiangsu lead); coal ramp-up as secondary; demand response in industrial sector; throttle LNG re-exports to retain domestic supply. 1 GW hydro down ≈ 7 spot LNG cargoes.

Trucked LNG is China's "last mile" solution for off-pipeline industrial parks, remote areas, and LNG-fueled vehicles. Two supply chains: ex-terminal (LNG from import terminals trucked inland — declining as import prices elevated) and land-based liquefaction plants (small-scale plants fed by CNPC pipeline gas or coke-oven gas in coal-heavy inland provinces — now 64% and growing). When import LNG is expensive, the market naturally shifts inland, insulating China from spot price shocks.

Demand growth drivers (bullish)
Industrial coal-to-gas switching

15th FYP carbon peaking 2030 mandate forces heavy industry away from coal boilers.

Gas-fired power additions (~20 GW/yr)

Peaker role grows as intermittent renewables expand; flexibility premium rises.

LNG trucking expansion

HDV fleet still has large penetration runway; LNG cheaper than diesel when spot prices moderate.

Urbanisation & city-gas connections

Continued new residential connections in tier-3/4 cities drives structural residential demand growth.

Demand headwinds (bearish)
Renewable cannibalization

Wind + solar capacity growing 30%+ annually; structural displacement of gas in baseload power post-2030.

GDP growth deceleration

Real estate downturn + trade uncertainty suppress industrial demand; structural rather than cyclical.

High spot LNG prices → demand destruction

China increasingly opts for pipeline gas, storage draw, or coal when spot LNG exceeds $15/MMBtu.

Power of Siberia-2 risk

If fast-tracked, POS-2 (~50 Bcm/yr) could displace 30–40 MMt of LNG imports between 2029 and 2035.

Four monthly windows are disproportionately important for forecasting China's full-year LNG import volume. Re-export volumes out of Chinese terminals remain the most transparent real-time signal of domestic demand slack — when China is long gas it re-exports within days; when tight, re-exports collapse within weeks.
Jan–Feb
Winter close-out

Watch: Temperature deviation (HDD index); North terminal utilisation; UGS withdrawal rate.

Bull signal: Cold snap → >3 Bcm/month excess withdrawal → spot LNG purchases activated → imports recover to 6+ mmt/month.

Bear signal: Warm LNY → imports fall to 3–4 mmt/month → re-exports spike above 0.8 mmt/month → full-year base eroded in H1.

Apr–May
Injection season pace

Watch: Manufacturing PMI (target >50); gas injection pace vs 5-year average; industrial YOY gas demand.

Bull signal: PMI >50.5; injection on pace; industrial gas growing YOY → strong H2 baseline set.

Bear signal: PMI <49.5; slow injection rate; industrial contracting → market long → spot purchases suppressed through summer.

Jul–Sep
Summer power peak — hydro vs gas battle

Watch: Yangtze River water level index; Sichuan hydro output vs norm; Guangdong power pool LNG consumption.

Bull signal: Hydro shortfall >10% → +6–8 Bcm gas-fired demand → Guangdong LNG tight → spot purchases spike. Largest single intra-year swing (1 GW hydro miss ≈ 7 spot cargoes in peak weeks).

Bear signal: Wet summer → hydro excess → gas-fired power curtailed → South terminal utilisation drops below 45% → re-exports activated.

Oct–Nov
Pre-winter build — make or break for full-year

Watch: UGS storage fill rate vs target; industrial PMI trend; trucked LNG price spread vs diesel.

Bull signal: Storage on track; industrial recovering; terminal bookings rising; winter premium building.

Bear signal: Storage behind target; industrial still weak; trucked LNG oversupplied; term contract diversion requests rising. If China ends Oct with imports still running below 5 mmt/month, full-year LNG imports will likely fall below 60 mmt — a multi-year low.

Analyst view
Near-term 2026

Market is well-supplied on contracted volumes but structurally weak on spot demand. Base case: ~63 mmt net imports — second consecutive annual decline, a first in China's history. A cold Q4 2026 combined with any Hormuz escalation is the most likely catalyst for a sharp LNG import recovery before year-end.

Medium-term 2027

Contract ramp-up from Qatar, US, Australian projects reduces spot exposure. Power sector capacity additions provide a structural demand floor. Key risk: if industrial recovery remains elusive and property drag deepens, industrial gas stays 5–8% below 2024 peak. Without new contract signings by 2026–27, the supply gap widens materially from 2029–30.
